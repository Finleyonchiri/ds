Bible Story

David and Goliath

In the Valley of Elah, the Israelites faced a formidable enemy: the Philistines, led by the giant Goliath. Goliath, over nine feet tall, taunted the Israelites daily, challenging them to send a champion to fight him. The Israelites were terrified, but a young shepherd named David stepped forward. Armed with only a sling, five smooth stones, and unwavering faith in God, David confronted Goliath. With a single stone, David struck Goliath in the forehead, felling the giant, and the Philistines fled in defeat.

Bible Reference: 1 Samuel 17:1-51

Lessons Learned:

  • Faith in God overcomes fear: David’s trust in God gave him courage to face a seemingly impossible challenge.
  • God uses the humble: A young shepherd defeated a giant, showing that God works through those who trust Him, regardless of their status.
  • Preparation matters: David’s experience as a shepherd prepared him to protect his flock—and God’s people—with skill and bravery.

Lesson 4: The Nations: Part 1 (April 19–25, 2025)

This week’s lesson, part of the series "Allusions, Images, Symbols: How to Study Bible Prophecy," explores the prophetic vision of Daniel 2, detailing the rise and fall of world empires and the establishment of God’s eternal kingdom. Through historical and biblical lenses, it contrasts human ambition with divine sovereignty, offering practical insights for living as citizens of God’s kingdom. Below is a detailed daily summary with key verses in the King James Version, enriched with theological and practical reflections.

Sabbath, April 19, 2025: The Rise of Human Empires

The lesson begins with Genesis 10:1-12, which outlines the descendants of Noah and the formation of nations post-Flood. This passage introduces the concept of human empire-building, epitomized by Nimrod, described as a “mighty hunter before the Lord” who founded Babel, the precursor to Babylon. The narrative sets the stage for Daniel 2’s prophetic vision, where earthly kingdoms, symbolized by the statue’s metals, are shown to be temporary and rooted in human pride. This contrast between human ambition and God’s eternal kingdom invites believers to prioritize divine values over worldly achievements.

Nimrod’s establishment of Babel and other cities reflects humanity’s desire for self-glorification, as seen in the later Tower of Babel narrative (Genesis 11:4). This ambition leads to division and conflict, a pattern repeated in the empires of Daniel 2—Babylon, Media-Persia, Greece, and Rome. The lesson draws parallels to modern societies, where nations and individuals often seek power and recognition at the expense of righteousness. It challenges believers to reflect on their own pursuits, asking whether they align with the fleeting glory of human empires or the enduring purpose of God’s kingdom.

Theologically, Genesis 10 underscores God’s sovereignty over the nations, as He orchestrates their rise and fall according to His plan. Practically, the lesson encourages believers to engage in their communities with humility, recognizing that true significance comes from serving God’s purposes. By studying the historical context, students gain a deeper understanding of prophecy as a revelation of God’s control over history, fostering trust in His ultimate victory. Key Verse: “And these are the sons of Ham, after their families, after their tongues, in their countries, and in their nations” (Genesis 10:20, KJV).

Sunday, April 20, 2025: God’s Call to a Nation

Genesis 12:1-9 recounts God’s call to Abraham to leave Ur and become the father of a nation that would bless all peoples. Unlike the self-serving empires of Genesis 10, Abraham’s nation is divinely ordained, rooted in faith and obedience. This narrative foreshadows the eternal kingdom of Daniel 2:44, symbolized by the stone “not cut by human hands,” which grows to fill the earth. The lesson emphasizes that God’s plan for humanity is redemptive, contrasting human ambition with divine purpose.

Abraham’s journey required immense trust, as he left his homeland without knowing his destination. This faith mirrors the certainty of God’s kingdom in Daniel 2, which is established by divine power, not human effort. The lesson explores how Abraham’s covenant (Genesis 12:3) extends to all believers, who are called to be a “spiritual Israel” that shares God’s blessings globally. This perspective challenges modern believers to prioritize their spiritual identity over national or cultural allegiances, living as ambassadors of God’s kingdom in a divided world.

Practically, the lesson invites reflection on how faith shapes personal mission. Just as Abraham was called to bless others, believers are urged to share God’s love through acts of kindness, evangelism, and advocacy for justice. Theologically, the study connects Abraham’s call to the broader biblical narrative, where God’s promises culminate in Christ’s kingdom. This hope inspires believers to persevere in faith, trusting in God’s plan despite worldly challenges. Key Verse: “And I will make of thee a great nation, and I will bless thee, and make thy name great; and thou shalt be a blessing” (Genesis 12:2, KJV).

Monday, April 21, 2025: The Dangers of Human Government

1 Samuel 8:4-18 describes Israel’s demand for a king to be “like all the nations,” rejecting God’s theocratic rule. Samuel warns of the consequences—oppressive taxation, conscription, and loss of freedom—revealing the flaws of human governance when it prioritizes power over righteousness. The lesson connects this to Daniel 2’s statue, where empires like Babylon and Rome rise through conquest but fall due to moral and spiritual decay, highlighting the impermanence of human systems.

Israel’s desire for a king stemmed from a lack of trust in God’s leadership and a desire to conform to worldly models. This mirrors the empires in Daniel 2, whose materials (gold, silver, bronze, iron, and clay) deteriorate, symbolizing declining integrity. The lesson challenges believers to evaluate the systems they rely on—political, economic, or social—and recognize their limitations compared to God’s perfect rule. It also explores how human governments often prioritize self-interest, contrasting with the justice and mercy of God’s kingdom.

Practically, the lesson calls for discernment in engaging with human institutions, encouraging believers to advocate for righteousness while maintaining allegiance to God. Theologically, it underscores God’s sovereignty over history, as seen in His judgment on earthly powers. The discussion prompts reflection on how faith can guide decisions in a world driven by power, fostering a commitment to live as citizens of God’s eternal kingdom. Key Verse: “Nevertheless the people refused to obey the voice of Samuel; and they said, Nay, but we will have a king over us” (1 Samuel 8:19, KJV).

Tuesday, April 22, 2025: Christ’s Model of Leadership

Matthew 20:25-28 presents Jesus’ teaching on servant-leadership, contrasting it with the domineering style of Gentile rulers. Jesus calls His disciples to serve rather than dominate, exemplifying this through His life and death. The lesson ties this to Daniel 2’s empires, which rely on conquest and control, while God’s kingdom, symbolized by the stone, is built on humility and sacrifice, offering a transformative model for leadership.

In the Roman context, leadership meant power and privilege, as seen in the empires of Daniel 2. Jesus redefines greatness, declaring that “whosoever will be great among you, let him be your minister.” This principle challenges the values of Babylon, Media-Persia, and Rome, whose rulers sought glory through oppression. The lesson explores how Christ’s example—washing His disciples’ feet and dying for humanity—sets the standard for believers, urging them to lead with compassion and selflessness in all spheres of life.

Practically, the lesson encourages believers to apply servant-leadership in their families, workplaces, and churches, fostering communities rooted in love. Theologically, it connects Christ’s leadership to the eternal kingdom, which triumphs through sacrifice, not force. The discussion prompts reflection on how to emulate Jesus’ example, offering hope that such leadership can transform societies and prepare believers for God’s kingdom. Key Verse: “But Jesus called them unto him, and said, Ye know that the princes of the Gentiles exercise dominion over them… But it shall not be so among you” (Matthew 20:25-26, KJV).

Wednesday, April 23, 2025: The Fall of Babylon

Revelation 18:1-4 calls God’s people to “come out of Babylon,” symbolizing corrupt worldly systems that oppose God’s truth. In Daniel 2, Babylon’s head of gold falls, signifying the transient nature of human empires. The lesson explores how Babylon represents not only the historical empire but also modern systems—cultural, economic, or religious—that prioritize human glory over divine will, urging believers to separate from such influences.

Revelation 18 depicts Babylon’s moral decay, materialism, and persecution of the faithful, mirroring the weaknesses of Daniel 2’s empires. The call to “come out” is both a warning and an invitation to align with God’s kingdom, which is eternal and just. The lesson examines how believers can identify “Babylon” in their lives, such as through consumerism, secular ideologies, or compromised values, and choose faithfulness to God’s principles instead.

Practically, the lesson challenges believers to live counter-culturally, reflecting God’s values in their choices and relationships. Theologically, it offers hope in God’s judgment on Babylon, assuring the triumph of His kingdom. The discussion encourages group reflection on supporting one another in resisting worldly pressures, fostering a community prepared for Christ’s return. Key Verse: “And I heard another voice from heaven, saying, Come out of her, my people, that ye be not partakers of her sins” (Revelation 18:4, KJV).

Thursday, April 24, 2025: The Eternal Kingdom

Daniel 2:44-45 describes the stone that destroys the statue and becomes a mountain, symbolizing God’s eternal kingdom. Unlike the human empires of gold, silver, bronze, iron, and clay, this kingdom is indestructible, established by divine power. The lesson highlights the hope this vision offers, assuring believers that God’s rule will ultimately replace all earthly powers, fulfilling His promises throughout Scripture.

The stone, representing Christ and His kingdom, grows to fill the earth, symbolizing the universal reign of God’s righteousness. The lesson connects this to the covenants with Abraham and David, where God promised a kingdom that would bless all nations. Theologically, it underscores the certainty of God’s prophetic timeline, encouraging believers to live with confidence in His victory. Historically, the succession of empires in Daniel 2 validates the accuracy of biblical prophecy, strengthening faith in God’s Word.

Practically, the lesson calls believers to embody the values of the eternal kingdom—love, justice, and humility—in their daily lives. It prompts reflection on how to share this hope through evangelism and service, preparing others for Christ’s return. The discussion fosters anticipation for the day when the stone fully manifests, uniting believers in their mission to reflect God’s kingdom now. Key Verse: “And in the days of these kings shall the God of heaven set up a kingdom, which shall never be destroyed” (Daniel 2:44, KJV).

Friday, April 25, 2025: Further Thought

Daniel 7:14 parallels Daniel 2, describing the Son of Man receiving an everlasting dominion that includes all peoples and nations. The lesson reinforces the certainty of God’s prophetic plan, where human empires give way to divine rule. This study encourages believers to trust in God’s sovereignty, living as faithful citizens of His kingdom amidst a world of temporary powers.

Theologically, Daniel 7:14’s universal scope highlights the inclusive nature of God’s kingdom, inviting all to serve Christ. The lesson draws from Ellen G. White’s writings, which emphasize studying prophecy to strengthen faith and prepare for Christ’s return. This perspective grounds the week’s study in SDA theology, urging believers to deepen their understanding of Scripture and its relevance to current events. The discussion explores how prophecy shapes a hopeful worldview, countering despair with assurance of God’s victory.

Practically, the lesson encourages group discussion on applying the hope of God’s kingdom through prayer, Bible study, and sharing the gospel. It prompts personal reflection on aligning priorities with eternal values, fostering a commitment to God’s mission. The study concludes with a call to live in anticipation of Christ’s return, actively preparing for the eternal kingdom. Key Verse: “And there was given him dominion, and glory, and a kingdom, that all people, nations, and languages, should serve him” (Daniel 7:14, KJV).

28 Fundamental Beliefs of the Seventh-day Adventist Church

Seventh-day Adventists accept the Bible as their only creed, holding 28 fundamental beliefs as the teaching of the Holy Scriptures. These beliefs, organized into six categories—God, Man, Salvation, Church, Christian Life, and Last-Day Events—express the church’s understanding of God’s Word. Guided by the Holy Spirit, these beliefs may be revised at a General Conference session to reflect a fuller understanding of Bible truth. They permeate every aspect of life, guiding Adventists to live in harmony with God’s will and prepare for Christ’s return.

[](https://www.adventist.org/beliefs/)

1. The Holy Scriptures

The Holy Scriptures, Old and New Testaments, are the inspired Word of God, written by holy men moved by the Holy Spirit. They are the infallible revelation of God’s will, providing the knowledge necessary for salvation. The Bible serves as the standard of character, the test of experience, and the authoritative source of doctrine. Adventists rely on Scripture to understand God’s plan, trusting its historical and prophetic accuracy. Practically, this belief encourages daily Bible study to align one’s life with divine truth. Key Verse: “All scripture is given by inspiration of God, and is profitable for doctrine, for reproof, for correction, for instruction in righteousness” (2 Timothy 3:16, KJV).

[](https://www.greenwichadventist.org.uk/28-fundamental-beliefs/)

2. The Trinity

There is one God: Father, Son, and Holy Spirit, a unity of three co-eternal Persons. God is immortal, all-powerful, all-knowing, and ever-present, yet known through His self-revelation. This doctrine affirms God’s infinite nature and His desire for a personal relationship with humanity. Adventists worship the Triune God, recognizing each Person’s role in creation, redemption, and sanctification. This belief shapes a holistic faith, emphasizing God’s love and unity. Key Verse: “Go ye therefore, and teach all nations, baptizing them in the name of the Father, and of the Son, and of the Holy Ghost” (Matthew 28:19, KJV).

[](https://www.adventist.org/trinity/)

3. The Father

God the Father is the Creator, Sustainer, and Sovereign of all creation. He is just, holy, merciful, and abounding in love and faithfulness. His unconditional love is revealed through His role in the Godhead, offering salvation through His Son. Adventists view the Father as a loving parent who desires intimacy with His children. This belief fosters trust in God’s provision and guidance, encouraging believers to approach Him in prayer. Key Verse: “But to us there is but one God, the Father, of whom are all things, and we in him” (1 Corinthians 8:6, KJV).

[](https://www.adventist.org/father/)

4. The Son

Jesus Christ, the eternal Son, is fully God and became incarnate to save humanity. He lived a sinless life, died for our sins, rose again, and ascended to heaven, where He intercedes for believers. His life and teachings reveal God’s character and provide the path to salvation. Adventists center their faith on Jesus, trusting His redemptive work. This belief inspires worship and emulation of Christ’s selfless love. Key Verse: “For God so loved the world, that he gave his only begotten Son, that whosoever believeth in him should not perish, but have everlasting life” (John 3:16, KJV).

[](https://www.hemetsda.org/28-fundamental-beliefs)

5. The Holy Spirit

The Holy Spirit, the third Person of the Godhead, inspired the Scriptures, empowers believers, and guides them into truth. He convicts of sin, comforts, and sanctifies, enabling a Christlike life. Adventists rely on the Spirit for spiritual growth and mission. This belief encourages openness to divine guidance and active participation in God’s work. Key Verse: “But the Comforter, which is the Holy Ghost, whom the Father will send in my name, he shall teach you all things” (John 14:26, KJV).

[](https://www.nadadventist.org/about-our-church/beliefs)

6. Creation

God created the universe in six literal days, culminating in the Sabbath. Humanity, made in God’s image, was the pinnacle of creation. Adventists affirm a recent creation, rejecting evolutionary theories, and view creation as evidence of God’s love and power. This belief shapes a worldview that values stewardship of the earth and human dignity. Key Verse: “In the beginning God created the heaven and the earth” (Genesis 1:1, KJV).

[](https://www.hemetsda.org/28-fundamental-beliefs)

7. The Nature of Humanity

Humans were created in God’s image, with free will and dominion over the earth. Sin caused a fall, introducing mortality and separation from God. Despite this, humans retain dignity and potential for redemption. Adventists emphasize holistic care for body, mind, and soul, reflecting God’s original design. This belief encourages respect for all people and personal growth in Christ. Key Verse: “So God created man in his own image, in the image of God created he him” (Genesis 1:27, KJV).

[](https://www.hemetsda.org/28-fundamental-beliefs)

8. The Great Controversy

All humanity is involved in a cosmic conflict between Christ and Satan, concerning God’s character and law. Originating in heaven with Satan’s rebellion, this controversy affects earth, where sin and suffering result from disobedience. God’s love is vindicated through Christ’s victory, and Adventists participate by sharing this truth. This belief provides hope and purpose amid evil. Key Verse: “And there was war in heaven: Michael and his angels fought against the dragon” (Revelation 12:7, KJV).

[](https://www.hemetsda.org/28-fundamental-beliefs)

9. The Life, Death, and Resurrection of Christ

Jesus lived a sinless life, died as a substitute for humanity’s sins, and rose from the dead, securing victory over sin and death. His resurrection guarantees eternal life for believers. Adventists celebrate Christ’s atoning work, which reconciles humanity to God. This belief inspires gratitude and confidence in salvation. Key Verse: “But he was wounded for our transgressions, he was bruised for our iniquities” (Isaiah 53:5, KJV).

[](https://www.hemetsda.org/28-fundamental-beliefs)

10. The Experience of Salvation

Salvation is a gift of grace, received through faith in Christ’s atoning sacrifice. The Holy Spirit regenerates believers, enabling a life of obedience and love. Adventists view salvation as both justification and sanctification, transforming lives to reflect God’s character. This belief fosters assurance and a commitment to spiritual growth. Key Verse: “For by grace are ye saved through faith; and that not of yourselves: it is the gift of God” (Ephesians 2:8, KJV).

[](https://www.hemetsda.org/28-fundamental-beliefs)

11. Growing in Christ

Through the Holy Spirit, believers grow in Christ, overcoming sin and reflecting His character. This growth involves prayer, Bible study, and service, fostering peace and freedom from fear. Adventists emphasize continual spiritual development, preparing for Christ’s return. This belief encourages a dynamic, transformative faith. Key Verse: “But grow in grace, and in the knowledge of our Lord and Saviour Jesus Christ” (2 Peter 3:18, KJV).

[](https://www.hemetsda.org/28-fundamental-beliefs)

12. The Church

The church is the community of believers who confess Jesus as Lord, united for worship, fellowship, and mission. It continues the work of God’s people from Old Testament times, proclaiming the gospel globally. Adventists value the church as a family, supporting spiritual growth and service. This belief fosters community and purpose. Key Verse: “And he is the head of the body, the church: who is the beginning, the firstborn from the dead” (Colossians 1:18, KJV).

[](https://www.adventist.org/the-church/)

13. The Remnant and Its Mission

The remnant church, faithful to God’s commandments, proclaims the gospel and the three angels’ messages of Revelation 14, preparing the world for Christ’s return. Adventists see themselves as part of this remnant, called to uphold truth and warn of judgment. This belief inspires evangelistic zeal and fidelity to Scripture. Key Verse: “And the dragon was wroth with the woman, and went to make war with the remnant of her seed” (Revelation 12:17, KJV).

[](https://www.hemetsda.org/28-fundamental-beliefs)

14. Unity in the Body of Christ

The church is one body, united in Christ despite cultural or ethnic differences. The Holy Spirit fosters unity through love and truth, enabling believers to reflect God’s character. Adventists promote inclusivity and collaboration in mission. This belief encourages harmony and mutual support within the global church. Key Verse: “That they all may be one; as thou, Father, art in me, and I in thee” (John 17:21, KJV).

[](https://www.hemetsda.org/28-fundamental-beliefs)

15. Baptism

Baptism by immersion symbolizes repentance, forgiveness, and union with Christ’s death and resurrection. It is a public declaration of faith, marking entry into the church. Adventists practice baptism for those who consciously accept Jesus, emphasizing its spiritual significance. This belief fosters commitment to a new life in Christ. Key Verse: “Therefore we are buried with him by baptism into death” (Romans 6:4, KJV).

[](https://www.learnreligions.com/seventh-day-adventist-beliefs-701396)

16. The Lord’s Supper

The Lord’s Supper commemorates Christ’s sacrifice, with bread and wine symbolizing His body and blood. It includes foot-washing, signifying humility and service. Open to all believers, it renews commitment to Jesus. Adventists observe this ordinance regularly, fostering unity and gratitude. Key Verse: “This do in remembrance of me” (Luke 22:19, KJV).

[](https://www.hemetsda.org/28-fundamental-beliefs)

17. Spiritual Gifts and Ministries

The Holy Spirit bestows spiritual gifts on believers for building up the church and spreading the gospel. These gifts, like teaching and healing, equip members for ministry. Adventists value diverse gifts, encouraging their use in service. This belief promotes active participation in God’s mission. Key Verse: “But the manifestation of the Spirit is given to every man to profit withal” (1 Corinthians 12:7, KJV).

[](https://www.hemetsda.org/28-fundamental-beliefs)

18. The Gift of Prophecy

The gift of prophecy, a mark of the remnant church, provides guidance through inspired messages. Adventists recognize Ellen G. White’s writings as prophetic, illuminating Scripture without replacing it. Her counsel supports spiritual growth and mission. This belief encourages discernment and reliance on divine guidance. Key Verse: “And the spirits of the prophets are subject to the prophets” (1 Corinthians 14:32, KJV).

[](https://www.hemetsda.org/28-fundamental-beliefs)

19. The Law of God

The Ten Commandments reflect God’s character and are binding for all people. They guide moral conduct, revealing sin and pointing to Christ’s righteousness. Adventists uphold the law, not for salvation, but as an expression of love for God. This belief fosters obedience and ethical living. Key Verse: “If ye love me, keep my commandments” (John 14:15, KJV).

[](https://www.hemetsda.org/28-fundamental-beliefs)

20. The Sabbath

The seventh-day Sabbath (Saturday) is a gift of rest and worship, instituted at creation and reaffirmed in the Ten Commandments. It commemorates God’s creative and redemptive acts. Adventists observe the Sabbath from sunset Friday to sunset Saturday, fostering spiritual renewal. This belief shapes lifestyle and priorities. Key Verse: “Remember the sabbath day, to keep it holy” (Exodus 20:8, KJV).

[](https://www.hemetsda.org/28-fundamental-beliefs)

21. Stewardship

Believers are stewards of God’s resources, including time, talents, and the environment. Stewardship reflects gratitude and responsibility, supporting God’s work through tithes and offerings. Adventists practice sustainable living and generosity, honoring God’s ownership. This belief encourages purposeful, disciplined living. Key Verse: “Moreover it is required in stewards, that a man be found faithful” (1 Corinthians 4:2, KJV).

[](https://www.hemetsda.org/28-fundamental-beliefs)

22. Christian Behavior

Christians are called to live Christlike lives, reflecting God’s love in actions and words. This includes modesty, healthful living, and avoiding harmful substances. Adventists strive for holiness, guided by Scripture, to honor God. This belief shapes daily choices and community interactions. Key Verse: “Whether therefore ye eat, or drink, or whatsoever ye do, do all to the glory of God” (1 Corinthians 10:31, KJV).

[](https://www.hemetsda.org/28-fundamental-beliefs)

23. Marriage and the Family

God instituted marriage as a lifelong union between a man and a woman, and the family as a reflection of His love. Adventists uphold the sanctity of marriage and prioritize nurturing families. This belief fosters strong relationships and community support for families. Key Verse: “Therefore shall a man leave his father and his mother, and shall cleave unto his wife” (Genesis 2:24, KJV).

[](https://www.hemetsda.org/28-fundamental-beliefs)

24. Christ’s Ministry in the Heavenly Sanctuary

Jesus ministers in the heavenly sanctuary, interceding for believers and applying His atoning sacrifice. Since 1844, He has engaged in an investigative judgment, reviewing the lives of the professed faithful. Adventists trust Christ’s priestly work, which assures salvation. This belief emphasizes accountability and preparation for judgment. Key Verse: “We have such an high priest, who is set on the right hand of the throne of the Majesty in the heavens” (Hebrews 8:1, KJV).

[](https://www.hemetsda.org/28-fundamental-beliefs)

25. The Second Coming of Christ

Christ’s second coming is literal, visible, and imminent, marking the climax of salvation. Believers will be resurrected or translated to meet Him, while the unrighteous face judgment. Adventists live in anticipation, sharing this hope globally. This belief inspires urgency and faithfulness. Key Verse: “And, behold, I come quickly; and my reward is with me” (Revelation 22:12, KJV).

[](https://www.hemetsda.org/28-fundamental-beliefs)

26. Death and Resurrection

Death is a state of unconsciousness, or “soul sleep,” until the resurrection at Christ’s return. The righteous are raised to eternal life, while the wicked face final judgment. Adventists reject immediate afterlife, trusting God’s timing. This belief offers comfort and hope in mortality. Key Verse: “For the living know that they shall die: but the dead know not any thing” (Ecclesiastes 9:5, KJV).

[](https://www.learnreligions.com/seventh-day-adventist-beliefs-701396)

27. The Millennium and the End of Sin

After Christ’s return, the righteous reign with Him in heaven for 1,000 years, while the earth lies desolate. At the millennium’s end, the wicked are resurrected, judged, and destroyed with Satan. Sin is eradicated, and God’s kingdom is established. This belief assures ultimate justice. Key Verse: “And I saw the dead, small and great, stand before God” (Revelation 20:12, KJV).

[](https://www.hemetsda.org/28-fundamental-beliefs)

28. The New Earth

God will create a new heaven and earth, where the redeemed live eternally with Him. Free from sin and suffering, the new earth restores Eden’s perfection. Adventists anticipate this eternal home, living in hope and service. This belief inspires a forward-looking faith. Key Verse: “And I saw a new heaven and a new earth: for the first heaven and the first earth were passed away” (Revelation 21:1, KJV).

Church History

The Seventh-day Adventist Church has a rich history rooted in the Great Awakening of the 19th century. Learn about our origins, growth, and global impact.

The Millerite Movement (1830s-1840s)

The SDA Church traces its roots to the Millerite movement, led by William Miller, who preached the imminent return of Christ based on Daniel 8:14.

The Great Disappointment (1844)

After Christ did not return on October 22, 1844, a group of believers studied further and discovered the sanctuary doctrine, leading to the formation of the SDA Church.

Official Organization (1863)

The Seventh-day Adventist Church was officially organized on May 21, 1863, with a focus on Sabbath-keeping, health reform, and missionary work.

Ellen G. White’s Ministry (1844-1915)

Ellen G. White, one of the church’s founders, provided spiritual guidance through her prophetic writings, which continue to inspire Adventists today.

Global Expansion (20th Century)

The church grew rapidly, establishing schools, hospitals, and publishing houses worldwide, becoming a global movement with millions of members.
